What is this?

It is a very basic text generator, to demonstrate simple processes that produce useful results. It uses a Markov chain to ingests a body of text and randomly generate a following token selected from the possible connections present in the digested text.

What's the point?

I thought it would be interesting to demo a proof-of-concept in the order of 50 lines of code. There are many examples out there but few are visually obvious. The goal, in short, is to show that not every task needs advanced AI!

Additionally, this code is easy to tweak, and adjusting tone of the output requires only changing out the relatively small amount of text needed for a flexible result. So a Markov-chain text generator can be put in place quickly, easily, and at effectively zero cost.

But it's not very good!

Yeah, it's not really supposed to be.

Markov generators have a number of practical applications. Realistic text generation is not one of them. But it does demonstrate the old but easily forgotten concept that simple algorithms have an important place in the programmatic toolkit. A Markov-chain generator won't be creative like a GPT, but a GPT would be ungainly if applied to contexts with extremely narrow output vectors.

In fact the very controversy of Bing Chat and other GPTs that have "gotten in trouble" highlights the desirability of narrow-scope solutions. Advanced predictive models have a high degree of freedom, and this leads to unwanted behaviors that are sometimes called "unpredictable" but are in fact very predictable, because they are modeled on the traits of human culture! It's much harder to get a damaging "hallucination" out of an algorithm that has no model for what language means.

Lastly, even when we achieve fast, efficient compute for LLMs, and resources no longer present a strain, there will always still be many uses for small, isolated, slimform iteration such as Markov chains (and other tools) can provide.
